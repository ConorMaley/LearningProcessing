Introduction

Hello! My name's Conor and I am about to graduate from the College of Wooster with a degree in Mathematics and Computer Science. I've had this file directory just sitting in my computer for the longest time called /Things I want to keep/code/, and it's basically a folder from the computer I used in high school of all of the code I wrote during my first year of programming. I learned how to program using a lightweight, visual-based programming language called Processing. After sifting through lines of elementary-level code and becoming nostalgic of my high school days, I though it would be cool if I used this site as a showcase or walkthrough of how I learned coding through Processing! You will see advances in not only programming techniques used but also in quality of material produced. Without further ado, let's begin.

The Very Beginning

Slightly Creepy Faces

First and foremost, "sketches" refers to a program running through Processing. One of the first things I did was making static drawings and exploring the user interactions that Processing has to offer. This here is smiley_face.pde. It's a pretty creepy looking, hard-coded smiley face made out of curves, ellipses, and lines. I guess I tried to make it do something when you click the screen because there is a mousePressed() function embedded in the code. I'm positive there are other drawings like this, but I didn't even bother saving them.

I continued with the idea of a smiley face and included more mouse interactions. The following is called drawing.pde. The user can draw lines by pressing down on the mouse and dragging. I think it was intended for users to complete the face, giving the two circles a nose, a mouth, and maybe even ears. I edited the code so that while active on the next sketch, you can press any key to reset the sketch.

After learning the basics of what can be done in Processing, I learned the power of for loops. The following is a static drawing of squares that change color gradiantly based on the location in the sketch.

Basic Animations

These first couple of sketches are pretty elementary and boring. Obviously I wanted to explore more exciting, dynamic features of Processing. This section discusses some basic animation and movements I explored.

Now begins the exciting stuff. The next sketch is called totalcontrol.pde. Use the WASD keys to move the circle around.

You could make the argument that the user is given total control over the circle... Ok, yeah I guess it's not that exciting. However, this inspired me to learn about movement and velocity! The next sketch is a simple example of a circle bouncing back and forth using velocity and direction variables.

And then I go ahead and combine the previous two! In the next sketch, you can move the circle around with the WASD keys and click the mouse to draw a line between the center of the circle and your mouse. If it gets too crazy, press the space bar to stop!

I think it's pretty cool how I combined two of the previous sketches into one. Making use of WASD control and velocity/direction movements. Next, I began touching on the power of classes, a very important concept in object-oriented programming. The next sketch uses a class to continuously expand and pop the bubble enclosing your mouse.

I didn't think classes were that important (even though they really are) since there aren't many other projects that I use classes in. The next two sketches are mainly animations that I expand on later, so remember these for later. The first is simply just a man running and jumping over a hurdle. It uses the modulo operator to animate the man's legs.

The next sketch also uses the modulo operator to animate. It's a rocket ship that follows the mouse, and when the user presses down on the mouse, the speed of the rocket ship increases. I actually commented in the code use your imagination. This time, I use the modulus operator to animate the exhaust given off by the rocket ship.

As you can see, the more projects I create, the more I am learning about simple Java programming. If interested, you can inspect the page to see how the code written advances through time. Coming up next, we begin developing simple games.

Simple Games and Maps

This is where things get really interesting, and I am not being sarcastic this time around. I wanted to learn how to program because I played so many video games and was curious how they worked. I begin developing simple games and generating maps in this section.

Remember the single hurdler from the previous section. Well, I gave him some friends and made them race. Using the power of arrays and random functions, and with the help of my teacher, the guys race and the sketch tells us who wins. Click on the screen to restart the race.

Not only is this an advancement of one of the previous pieces I created, but it also utilizes the power of arrays. The next sketch is another game, but the user actually takes part in it. It's called tie_fighter.pde and the goal is to destroy the ship in front of you. To destroy it, use the space bar to shoot your laser and the A and D keys to move back and forth. The ship takes six hits of the laser to get brought down, it's actually a bit difficult. Once you destroy it, press R to reset and play again. I used the previous idea of having the user flying through space, and I suppose I watched Star Wars that week for a bit of inspiration. I'll even include the print log so you can see how I was using simple print functions to debug my work.

Along with these games, I also created my rendition of the classic Snake game everyone knows and loves. Use the number keys to select your level (level 6 and up are very difficult, level 9 is almost impossible), and arrow keys to move. I had to make a couple of slight modifications since it wasn't working when running in the browser.

I remember feeling pretty satisfied after finishing the snake game. It took a lot of time and I used arrays, modulus, and various canvas manipulations to successfully create a simple game. Next I started exploring long if else statements. The next sketch creates rectangles of various size and colors randomly. As the rectangles overlap, it's almost like they paint the background over and over again.

I then become involved in this idea of map generation. The next two sketches create 8-bit-like, 2D maps. The first sketch treats the canvas like a 40x40 grid. There are 8 different climates, each denoted by a different color. It creates the map by moving through each 160 blocks of the grid, by columns then rows, using the block above and to the left of the current block to decide what kind of climate the current block is. In doing this the map isn't totally randomly generated, but rather has a perlin noise-like feel to the maps. Click the sketch to restart.

The next sketch is an upgrade of the previous. It is larger and still treats the map as a grid and decides what the block looks like based on its surroundings, but fills the map randomly. If there are any blocks not filled by the initial filling, the untouched blocks are finished with a water climate. Aesthetically, the climate colors are slightly different. Once again, click the sketch to restart.

After revisiting these works, I find that both are intended for you to use your imagination. Either the maps are extensions of a much bigger world or they may exist in their own dimension.

Later Works

I took a hiatus from Processing for a while but I revisited it recently and created the following sketches. The first is a simple piece of work inspired by the distortion of a water stream using electricity. Move your mouse close to the falling circles to make them move.

My most recent work completed in Processing can be found on my github page, here. It is a simulation of a visually-based predator-prey model where the predator and prey both change and evolve their traits to survive. I'll just link to the github page since I have experienced some problems when leaving the simulation running for too long, and since this is the last piece I'm showing off, it would be a great time for you to download Processing and the Evolutionary Ecosystem to learn the amazing environment Processing is! Here is just a gif of the simulation in action. The red dots are the predators and the grey are the prey. Eating increases the creatures' health, the predators eat the prey and the prey graze on the food (grey squares).
